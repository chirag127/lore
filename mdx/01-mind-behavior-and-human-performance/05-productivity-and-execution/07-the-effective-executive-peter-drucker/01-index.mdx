Peter Drucker, the father of modern management, wrote *The Effective Executive* to answer a question that is more relevant today than ever: what separates effective knowledge workers from the rest? His answer is that effectiveness is not a matter of intelligence, personality, or talent—it is a discipline, a set of practices that can be learned.

The book identifies five practices that make an executive effective: managing time, focusing on contribution, making strengths productive, setting priorities, and making effective decisions. Each practice is explained with Drucker's characteristic insight, clarity, and practical wisdom. Despite being written in 1966, the book feels startlingly contemporary—the challenges of knowledge work have changed less than we think.
