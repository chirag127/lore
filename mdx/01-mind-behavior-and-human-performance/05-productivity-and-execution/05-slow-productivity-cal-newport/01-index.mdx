Cal Newport, who popularized "deep work" a decade ago, returns with a diagnosis of what's gone wrong in knowledge work—and a radical prescription. His target is "pseudo-productivity," the modern obsession with visible busyness: endless emails, Slack messages, meetings about meetings, and the performance of being constantly occupied. This busyness, he argues, is not productivity at all—it's a substitute for it.

The alternative is "slow productivity," a philosophy with three principles: do fewer things, work at a natural pace, and obsess over quality. Drawing on the working habits of great thinkers and makers—from Galileo to Maya Angelou—Newport shows that the most important work in history was produced not by people who were busy, but by people who gave themselves time for deep, unhurried thought.
