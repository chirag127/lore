# The Rise and Fall of the Great Powers

## Executive Summary

Paul Kennedy's The Rise and Fall of the Great Powers is a sweeping historical analysis of the relationship between economic power and military power from 1500 to the present. Kennedy's central thesis is that the relative power of great nations shifts over time as their economic bases grow or shrink, and that decline typically results from imperial overstretch — a situation in which a state's military commitments exceed its economic capacity to sustain them. The book was enormously influential when published and remains a classic of grand strategy.



## Key Takeaways

- **Economic power** is the foundation of military power and national influence
- **Imperial overstretch** drives the decline of great powers
- **Relative power shifts** as productivity and technology change
- **The US showed signs** of overstretch in the 1980s
- **No power remains dominant** indefinitely

## Who Should Read

- **Strategy students**: The classic text on grand strategy and national power
- **History lovers**: Five centuries of great power politics in one volume
- **Policy thinkers**: Historical perspective on American decline debates
- **Economics fans**: The economic foundations of military power

## Who Should Skip

- Those wanting **cultural or intellectual history**
- Readers seeking **deterministic theories**
- People looking for **specific policy prescriptions**

## Difficulty

**Medium** — Analytical but accessible. Some background in modern history helpful but not required.
