Annie Duke spent twenty years at the poker table, where every decision is literally a bet — chips in the middle, information incomplete, outcome determined by a mix of skill and luck. In *Thinking in Bets*, she translates the probabilistic mindset that separates elite poker players from amateurs into a framework anyone can use.



The core insight is deceptively simple: most of life's decisions are bets, not chess moves. A bet is a decision about an uncertain future. Once you internalize that, everything changes — how you evaluate past outcomes, how you plan for the future, and how you resist the cognitive biases that make us all terrible at probability. Duke's great contribution is not the idea itself but the toolkit she builds around it: bet-framing, the decision journal, the 10-10-10 rule, the pre-mortem, and the truth-seeking pod. These are not abstract principles — they are repeatable mental habits forged at the poker table and tested across thousands of high-stakes decisions.
