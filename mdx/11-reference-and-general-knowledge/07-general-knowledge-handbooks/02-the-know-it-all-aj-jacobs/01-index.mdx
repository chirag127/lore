# The Know-It-All

## Executive Summary

A.J. Jacobs, an editor at Esquire, decides to read the entire 32-volume Encyclopedia Britannica from A to Z and write about the experience. The result is a hilarious and surprisingly touching memoir about the pursuit of knowledge, the nature of intelligence, and what it means to try to know everything. Each chapter covers a letter of the alphabet, weaving Jacobs's personal journey with the strange and wonderful facts he discovers along the way.



## Key Takeaways

- **Reading the entire Britannica** takes about 1-2 years of dedicated effort
- **The experience reveals** fascinating connections across fields of knowledge
- **Total knowledge** is impossible, but the pursuit is worthwhile
- **Encyclopedias** are both treasure troves and products of their time
- **The process of learning** is often more valuable than the knowledge gained

## Who Should Read

- **Knowledge seekers**: Entertaining exploration of what it means to know things
- **Humorous memoir fans**: Jacobs's self-deprecating style is delightful
- **Book lovers**: Appreciate obsessive, quest-based narratives
- **Jacobs fans**: The first of his many wonderful quest books

## Who Should Skip

- Those seeking **systematic reference** works
- Readers who prefer **serious, non-humorous** treatments of knowledge
- People who dislike **self-deprecating memoir** style

## Difficulty

**Easy** — Engaging, humorous memoir accessible to all readers.

## Reading Time

- **Full Read**: 6-8 hours
