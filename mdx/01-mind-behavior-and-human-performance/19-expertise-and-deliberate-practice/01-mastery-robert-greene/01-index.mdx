What did Leonardo da Vinci, Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart, Albert Einstein, and Henry Ford have in common? According to Robert Greene, they all followed a similar path to mastery—one that is available to anyone willing to commit to a long-term process of development. *Mastery*, the fifth book from the author of *The 48 Laws of Power*, is a study of how genius is cultivated rather than born.

Greene argues that mastery unfolds in three phases: the apprenticeship (years of focused learning and skill building), the creative-active phase (applying those skills in original ways), and the final mastery phase (achieving intuitive, fluid expertise). Drawing on the lives of historical masters, he shows that the apprenticeship phase is where most people fall short—they crave shortcuts and instant recognition, not understanding that all great achievement rests on a foundation of disciplined, patient practice.

What distinguishes *Mastery* from other books on expertise is its scope and style. Greene does not offer quick tips or five-step programs. He offers a philosophy of lifelong learning, illustrated through vivid biographical case studies, and argues that the path to mastery is ultimately a path to self-actualization. The book is both a critique of modern culture's impatience and a roadmap for those willing to take the long view.
