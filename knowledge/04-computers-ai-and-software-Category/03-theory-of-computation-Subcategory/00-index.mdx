# 🧮 Theory of Computation

## 📚 Overview

What can be computed? What can be computed efficiently? And what are the fundamental limits of computation? The theory of computation answers these questions with mathematical rigor.

This shelf covers the core topics: automata theory, formal languages, computability, complexity classes, and the logical foundations of computation. It includes Gödel's proof, the halting problem, and the P vs. NP question.

These books are for the computer scientist who wants to understand the deepest foundations of the field.

## 🔗 Category Boundaries

- **Data & Algorithms** covers practical algorithm design.
- **Computer Science** covers programming language theory.
- **Computer Science Theory** covers information theory and compilers.

This category focuses on the **mathematical limits of computation** — what can and cannot be computed.

## 📖 Topics Covered

- Automata theory: finite automata, pushdown automata, Turing machines
- Formal languages and grammars (Chomsky hierarchy)
- Computability: decidability and the halting problem
- Computational complexity: P, NP, NP-completeness
- Gödel's incompleteness theorems
- Logic and foundations of computation

## ⭐ Essential Books

- **Gödel's Proof** — Nagel & Newman: An accessible introduction to one of the most profound results in mathematics.
- **Automata Theory** — The mathematical models of computation: finite automata, pushdown automata, and Turing machines.
- **Computability, Decidability, and the Halting Problem** — The fundamental limits of what computers can decide.
- **Computational Complexity Classes** — P, NP, and the question that keeps computer scientists up at night.
- **Formal Languages and Grammars** — The Chomsky hierarchy and the structure of formal languages.

## 🚀 Why This Category Matters

The theory of computation defines the boundaries of what's possible with computers. Understanding these limits helps you recognize which problems are tractable, which are intractable, and which are fundamentally unsolvable.
