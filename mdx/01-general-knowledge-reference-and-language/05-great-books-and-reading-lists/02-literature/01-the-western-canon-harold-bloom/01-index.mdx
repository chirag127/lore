# The Western Canon

## Executive Summary

Harold Bloom's The Western Canon is a passionate defense of the Western literary tradition against what he sees as the politicization of literary studies. The book presents Bloom's selection of 26 canonically central authors from Dante through Beckett, explains what makes their work canonical, and argues that aesthetic value — not social utility or political correctness — should determine what we read and teach.



## Key Takeaways

- **Shakespeare is the center** of the Western canon
- **Canonical works** are characterized by originality, complexity, and ability to reward rereading
- **The canon is constantly evolving** as new works enter and older works are revalued
- **Bloom identifies 26 canonical authors** from Dante to Beckett
- **Academic trends threaten** the canon's survival

## Who Should Read

- **Serious readers**: Understand what makes literature great
- **Literature students**: Bloom's critical framework
- **Educators**: Think about what works to teach
- **Anyone**: Why certain books are called "great"

## Who Should Skip

- Those who **reject the idea** of a literary canon
- **Diverse/multicultural** literature seekers
- Those who dislike **Bloom's combative style**

## Difficulty

**Hard** — Dense literary criticism requiring substantial literary knowledge. Bloom assumes familiarity with most works discussed.

## Reading Time

- **Full Read**: 12-15 hours

## Final Verdict

**Essential** for anyone serious about Western literature. Bloom's arguments are provocative and his opinions are strong, but the book is a masterclass in literary criticism. Whether you agree or disagree with Bloom, the encounter is valuable.
